When Courses are Taken

This visualization shows how courses are distributed throughout the day.

You can choose which value to plot:

  • Course Count: The number of courses offered at a given time.
  • Enrollment: The number of students expected to be in class.
  • Capacity: The maximum number of students that could be in class.

You can also filter by department. This excludes cross-listed courses listed as belonging to another department.

The "normalize outputs" option will scale each department's graph so that they are all the same height. This may make cross-department timing comparisons easier.



Subjects

Departments that have labs, such as chemistry or physics, tend to account for more enrollment late in the day compared to other departments. Language departments, on the other hand, offer significantly more classes during lunchtime, with Spanish and French contributing a significant portion during that time. There is a large gap from 4:30 to 7:30 where very few courses are in session. Architecture is a notable exception, offering half the courses available from 4:30 to 6:00. This stems from the fact that it offers many 500-level studio classes that run from 1:30 to 5:50.

Department Comparisons

This table summarizes several statistics about each department's course offerings. Click any column header to sort by that field.

Total Instruction Time refers to the total time classes are in session. If two classes are both being taught at the same time, both will contribute to the total. This is a measure of how large the "Course Count" graph above is.

Collective Student Class Time refers to the sum of the time students spend in class. Conceptually, this value can be thought of as being the hours of instruction weighted by enrollment. For example, if 12 students are taught for 3 hours, the collective class time would be 36. This is a measure of how large the "Enrollment" graph above is.

Efficiency is the Collective Class Time / Instruction Time, which reflects how many students, on average, are receiving instruction per hour of class. This captures that each hour of class for a 12 person class is going to result in 12 hours of learning across all students. This is a measure of how personalized the content is.

Average Time represents the duration-weighted average time at which courses take place.

An interesting comparison is between Near Eastern Studies and Global Health & Health Policy. Despite them both having around 9.8 days of collective student class time, Near Eastern Studies achieved it over 21 courses, while Global Health & Health Policy was able to in just one.

A strong correlation I found in this data is that departments offering more courses tended to have less variability in the average time courses are offered.

Course Number Distributions

This is the distribution of the course numbers chosen for classes. The gap at the end is the jump from 500- to 1000-level courses.

The most frequent course number was 498, appearing 38 times. This generally corresponds to the class Senior Thesis I, except in the cases of ART (Senior Thesis Development), ECO and GEO (Senior Thesis Foundation), ORF (Senior Independent Research Foundations), and PHY (Senior Thesis Planning).

The next most used numbers are 101 (30 instances), 105 (26), 501 (22), and then 301 (18).

The 1000-level courses correspond to the Introductory [Language] II (1002) courses offered by the Chinese, Japanese, and Korean departments.
